Flash floods are the most destructive natural hazard in Himachal Pradesh (HP), India,
causing over 400 fatalities and \$1.2 billion in losses in the 2023 monsoon season alone.
Existing susceptibility maps for the region cover only a single river basin,
inflate accuracy estimates using random train-test splits,
and report only a single risk score with no measure of uncertainty.
This study presents the first state-wide, multi-basin flash flood susceptibility assessment
for HP that addresses all three shortcomings.

We built a six-year flood inventory (2018--2023) from Sentinel-1 radar imagery
processed in Google Earth Engine, supplemented by terrain filtering to identify plausible flood locations,
yielding 3,000 georeferenced flood occurrences across HP.
Twelve environmental variables spanning terrain, hydrology, land cover, soil, and rainfall
were assembled at 30\,m resolution after removing one redundant pair ($|r|=0.97$).
Four machine-learning models served as pixel-based baselines: Random Forest, XGBoost,
LightGBM, and a stacking ensemble.
Our primary contribution is a Graph Neural Network (GraphSAGE) trained on a watershed
connectivity graph (460 sub-watersheds, 1,700 edges), which captures how flooding in
upstream catchments raises risk downstream --- something pixel-based models cannot represent.
All models were evaluated using leave-one-basin-out cross-validation to avoid the
5--15\% accuracy inflation common in random train-test splits.
We also applied conformal prediction to produce the first HP susceptibility maps
with statistically guaranteed 90\% coverage intervals,
enabling planners to distinguish high-risk zones where the model is confident
from those where uncertainty is high.

The GNN achieved a mean AUC of 0.995\,$\pm$\,0.004, outperforming the best
pixel-based model (stacking ensemble: AUC\,=\,0.901) and the published benchmark
of \citet{saha2023} (AUC\,=\,0.88).
Conformal intervals achieved 82.9\% coverage on a held-out 2023 test set,
with lower coverage in high-risk zones (45--59\%) due to noise in the SAR-derived labels.
Elevation, plan curvature, and slope were the three most important predictors overall,
but the dominant driver varied by district.
High-susceptibility zones overlap 1,457\,km of national highways, 2,759 bridges,
and 4 major hydroelectric installations --- exposures that are directly actionable
by the HP State Disaster Management Authority.

\noindent\textbf{Plain language summary:}
We built the most complete flood risk map yet produced for Himachal Pradesh
by training a model that understands how water flows through connected river valleys,
and by reporting not only \textit{where} flooding is likely
but \textit{how confident} the model is in each prediction.
