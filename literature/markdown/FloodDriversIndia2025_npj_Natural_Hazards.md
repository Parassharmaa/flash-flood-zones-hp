# Drivers of Flash Floods in the Indian Sub-Continental River Basins

**Authors:** (Authors from npj Natural Hazards, 2025)
**Year:** 2025
**Journal:** npj Natural Hazards (Nature Publishing Group)
**DOI/URL:** https://www.nature.com/articles/s44304-025-00121-3

## Study Area
Indian subcontinent — all major river basins analyzed at national/regional scale. Comparative analysis across different physiographic regions.

## Methods
Statistical analysis of historical flash flood records, hydrometeorological data, and geomorphic parameters to identify dominant drivers by region. Multi-variable analysis comparing geomorphological vs. hydrological controls.

## Conditioning Factors / Drivers Analyzed
- Geomorphological: slope, basin relief, drainage density, channel morphology
- Hydrological: "flashiness" index (rapid rise-and-fall of hydrograph), precipitation intensity
- Climatological: monsoon patterns, Western Disturbances, cyclonic systems
- Anthropogenic: land use change, river channel modifications

## Key Findings
1. **Flash flood hotspots are concentrated in three zones:** Himalayas, West Coast (Western Ghats), and Central India
2. **Regional driver differentiation:**
   - **Himalayas:** Geomorphological factors dominate (steep slopes, confined valleys, high relief)
   - **West Coast (Konkan, Western Ghats):** Hydrological factors dominate (high flashiness, extreme monsoon rainfall)
   - **Central India:** Combined geomorphological + hydrological
3. Himalayan flash floods are primarily terrain-controlled — steep gradients force rapid concentration of runoff
4. Increasing trend in flash flooding incidents over past decades — attributed to climate change effects
5. GLOF contribution in Himalayas is growing and increasingly significant

## Limitations
- National-scale analysis necessarily generalized
- Sub-regional variation within Himalayas (e.g., windward vs. rain shadow zones like Spiti) not fully resolved
- Historical record completeness varies across basins

## Relevance to HP Flash Flood Study
This Nature-published paper provides the scientific rationale for HP as a priority study area:
- Confirms Himalayas as a primary Indian flash flood hotspot
- Geomorphological factor dominance in Himalayan flash floods directly supports the conditioning factor framework for HP (slope, elevation, terrain derivatives)
- GLOF contribution growing confirms need for GLOF-integrated approach in HP
- The geomorphological driver finding means DEM-derived factors (slope, curvature, TRI, SPI) will be especially important in HP — more so than in coastal or plains settings
- Published in Nature journal → highly citable; citing it strengthens the justification for HP study
