# HiFlo-DAT: A Flood Hazard Event-Disaster Database for the Kullu District, Himachal Pradesh, Indian Himalaya

**Authors:** (University of Cumbria and collaborators; full author list in paper)
**Year:** 2025
**Journal:** International Journal of Disaster Risk Reduction (ScienceDirect)
**DOI/URL:** https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S2212420925001608

## Study Area
Kullu District, Himachal Pradesh, Indian Himalaya. The Beas River and its major tributaries (Parvati, Sainj, Tirthan rivers). Area spans high-Himalayan to lower montane zones.

## Methods
- Historical documentary archive research (global + regional collections)
- Over 160,000 English-language documentary records (1835–2020) systematically processed
- Community knowledge integration
- GIS mapping of flood event locations (59 unique locations)
- Timeline and seasonality analysis
- Cause attribution for each event

## Conditioning Factors (inventory-based)
Primary flood causes documented:
- Monsoon rainfall (dominant cause)
- Cloudbursts (localized extreme rainfall)
- Glacial Lake Outburst Floods (GLOFs)
- Landslide-dammed lake outbursts
- Snowmelt contribution (secondary)

## Performance
Not a predictive model — this is a historical disaster database.

## Key Findings
**Database Statistics:**
- **128 flood events** at **59 locations** over **175 years (1846–2020)**
- Substantially improves on existing compilations that document only 5–15 events since mid-1990s

**Temporal Patterns:**
- Two flood-rich periods: 1890s–1900s and 1990s–present
- Increasing flood occurrence toward present time (trend consistent with climate change)

**Spatial Patterns:**
- Repeated flooding concentrated in small number of Beas River tributaries
- Parvati and Sainj valleys particularly flood-prone

**Vulnerability Patterns:**
- Labourer-migrant communities (road/hydroelectric construction): 70% of fatalities, 83% of affected persons
- Key impact receptors: roads (55 floods), bridges (54 floods, 94 impacts)

**Causation:**
- Rainfall dominates; summer monsoon flooding predominates
- GLOF contribution significant in upper valley areas

## Limitations
- English-language archives only — may miss local-language records of smaller events
- Urban/infrastructure expansion since 1970s creates non-stationarity in exposure
- Historical damage records inconsistent across time periods
- Coverage biased toward valley settlements; high-mountain events undercounted

## Relevance to HP Flash Flood Study
This is the most important inventory dataset for Kullu District HP work:
- 128 flood events + 59 locations provides training/validation data for ML models
- The 175-year temporal coverage enables frequency-return period analysis
- Beas tributaries (Parvati, Sainj, Tirthan) are priority sub-basins for susceptibility mapping
- Road/bridge infrastructure as primary impact receptor means infrastructure vulnerability mapping is high-value output
- Labourer-migrant community vulnerability points to need for exposure/risk layer beyond just hazard
- A similar database methodology (HiFlo-DAT 2.0) is being prepared for Chamoli — highlights transferability of approach
- GLOF + monsoon + cloudburst multiple flood triggers means conditioning factors must capture all mechanisms
