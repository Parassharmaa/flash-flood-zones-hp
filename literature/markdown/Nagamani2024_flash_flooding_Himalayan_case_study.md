# Understanding Flash Flooding in the Himalayan Region: A Case Study

**Authors:** Katukotta Nagamani, Anoop Kumar Mishra, Mohammad Suhail Meer, Jayanta Das
**Year:** 2024
**Journal:** Scientific Reports
**DOI/URL:** https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10963777/

## Study Area
Nainital district, Uttarakhand, Indian Himalaya (28.44°–31.28°N, 77.35°–81.01°E). A mountainous region with steep slopes and dense human settlement. Experienced catastrophic October 2021 flash floods.

## Methods
- Meteosat-8 satellite data with rain index-based estimation techniques
- Statistical trend analysis: Mann-Kendall test, Modified Mann-Kendall, Sen's slope estimator
- Aerosol trajectory analysis via HYSPLIT (backward trajectory modeling)
- Remote sensing analysis of atmospheric parameters: aerosol optical depth (AOD), cloud thickness, precipitable water vapor
- Historical rainfall time series analysis (2000–2021)

## Conditioning Factors
- Total aerosol optical depth (AOD)
- Cloud cover thickness
- Total precipitable water vapor
- Elevation and topographic slope
- Air mass trajectories (western disturbance patterns)
- Monsoon dynamics

## Performance
Not applicable — meteorological/atmospheric analysis, not ML susceptibility mapping.

## Key Findings
1. October 2021 experienced unprecedented 270 mm rainfall over two days — anomalous for the region (normally dry in October)
2. Historical patterns: June–September is primary flood season; October event was extreme outlier
3. High aerosol optical depth (0.8) preceding the event enhanced cloud formation and rainfall
4. Steep Himalayan slopes "significantly correlate with casualty numbers" from flood events
5. Air mass trajectories from western regions (Western Disturbances) combined with monsoon moisture created exceptional precipitation
6. Over 300 casualties in Uttarakhand during 2021

## Limitations
- Limited ground-based rain gauge network in the study region
- Focus restricted to single October 2021 event
- Remote sensing rainfall estimates have accuracy issues over high-altitude terrain
- Does not produce susceptibility map — process analysis only

## Relevance to HP Flash Flood Study
- Confirms that HP/Uttarakhand experience flash floods both during and outside standard monsoon season (Western Disturbances in Oct–Nov)
- Aerosol optical depth as a precursor factor is novel — could be incorporated as a dynamic predictor
- Sparse gauge network finding directly applies to HP: satellite-based precipitation (CHIRPS, GPM-IMERG) needed
- Confirms that slope is a primary determinant of casualties in Himalayan flash floods
- The multi-season flood risk (not just June–September) is important context for HP study design
- Mann-Kendall trend analysis on precipitation time series is methodologically useful for establishing rainfall trends in HP
